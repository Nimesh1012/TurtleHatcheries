Turtle Species in Sri Lanka

Sri Lanka is famous for its lovely beaches and amazing wildlife. Among the many types of wildlife found in Sri Lanka, turtles are indeed one of the most attractive and precious. Out of the 7 types of turtles in the world, the sea of Sri Lanka provides habitat to 5 types. Among the five turtle species found in the Sri Lankan ocean, three are endangered, one is vulnerable and one is critically endangered. They visit the Sri Lankan shores to nest. In fact, there is a high population of sea turtles on the southern coast of Sri Lanka. So, they are in need of conservation. And that is the place where the turtle hatcheries come on stage.

Baby Turtles! Yes, this might be the first thing that comes to your mind when you hear about turtle hatcheries. But believe us, there is much more you will experience if you visit the turtle hatcheries on this beautiful island. Continue reading to know all about the turtles and turtles hatcheries in Sri Lanka!



Green Turtle (Chelonia mydas)


Green Turtles are the most common type of turtle in the ocean of Sri Lanka. They got their name because of the color of fat found under their upper shell. They can grow to a maximum length of 1m and can weigh around 250kg. Green Turtles have a smaller head and a pair of large flippers.

Adult female Green turtles lay between 120-140 eggs at one time. Their eggs take up to 48 days to hatch. They lay eggs several times in one season. But they will only nest once every few years. Young turtles are carnivorous while the grown green turtles feed on marine vegetation.

Where you have a chance to see Green turtles

  • Pigeon Island National Park: Located off the east coast of Sri Lanka near Nilaveli, Pigeon Island National Park is known for its vibrant coral reefs and diverse marine life, including Green turtles. Snorkeling or diving in the crystal-clear waters of Pigeon Island provides opportunities to spot these magnificent creatures.
  • Rekawa Beach: Situated on the south coast of Sri Lanka, Rekawa Beach is renowned for its turtle nesting activity. It is one of the most important nesting sites for sea turtles, including Green turtles, in the country. Conservation organizations such as the Rekawa Turtle Watch operate guided tours to observe nesting turtles during the nesting season.
  • Kosgoda Turtle Conservation Project: The Kosgoda Turtle Conservation Project, located in Kosgoda on the west coast of Sri Lanka, is dedicated to conserving sea turtles. The project focuses on protecting turtle nests, rescuing and rehabilitating injured turtles, and releasing them back into the ocean. Visitors can learn about turtle conservation efforts and have the opportunity to see Green turtles up close.
  • Bentota: Bentota, a popular beach destination on the southwest coast of Sri Lanka, is another area where Green turtles can be encountered. The tranquil waters of Bentota provide a favorable environment for turtles, and you may spot them while swimming or snorkeling in the area.

Hawksbill turtle


The Hawksbill turtle (Eretmochelys imbricata) is a critically endangered species of sea turtle known for its distinctive appearance. Named after its beak-like mouth that resembles that of a hawk, the Hawksbill turtle has a flattened body shape and a carapace with overlapping scales forming a serrated pattern. Their carapace displays a beautiful combination of colors including brown, amber, black, and orange. Adult Hawksbill turtles typically reach lengths of 2 to 3 feet and weigh around 100 to 150 pounds, although larger individuals can be found. These turtles inhabit tropical and subtropical waters of the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans, favoring coral reefs, rocky areas, lagoons, and coastal waters. Hawksbill turtles are primarily spongivorous, feeding on sponges, but they also consume other invertebrates such as jellyfish, sea anemones, shrimp, and squid. Their feeding habits play a crucial role in maintaining the health of coral reef ecosystems, as they prevent sponges from overgrowing and outcompeting corals. Unfortunately, Hawksbill turtles are facing numerous threats that have led to their critically endangered status. These include habitat loss, climate change, pollution, bycatch in fishing gear, and illegal wildlife trade for their beautiful tortoiseshell. Conservation efforts are vital to protect and preserve this remarkable species for future generations..


Where you have a chance to see Hawksbill turtles

  • Rekawa Beach: Located on the south coast of Sri Lanka, Rekawa Beach is renowned as one of the most important nesting sites for sea turtles in the country, including the Hawksbill Turtle. The Rekawa Turtle Conservation Project operates here, allowing visitors to witness the nesting and hatching process while ensuring the safety of the turtles.
  • Kosgoda Beach: Situated along the southwestern coast, Kosgoda Beach is another prominent nesting site for sea turtles, including the Hawksbill Turtle. The Kosgoda Turtle Conservation Project operates in this area, conducting research and conservation efforts while also providing educational opportunities for visitors.
  • Revi Karuna Karan Memorial Turtle Conservation Farm: Located in the town of Bentota on the southwestern coast, this turtle hatchery and conservation farm focuses on protecting and rehabilitating various species of sea turtles, including the Hawksbill Turtle. Visitors can learn about conservation efforts and even release baby turtles into the ocean.

Olive Ridley Turtle


The Olive Ridley Turtle (Lepidochelys olivacea) is a species of sea turtle found in various parts of the world, including Sri Lanka. These turtles are named for their olive-colored carapace, or shell. They are relatively small in size, with adults typically measuring about 2-2.5 feet (60-75 cm) in length and weighing around 75-100 pounds (34-45 kg). Olive Ridley Turtles have a distinctive heart-shaped shell and a relatively small head with a hooked beak. They have powerful flippers, which enable them to navigate through the ocean waters with agility.

These turtles have a primarily omnivorous diet, feeding on a variety of marine organisms such as jellyfish, shrimp, crabs, and algae. They are highly adapted to life in the ocean and are capable of diving to considerable depths in search of food.


Where you have a chance to see Olive Ridley Turtle

  • Rekawa Beach: Located on the south coast of Sri Lanka, Rekawa Beach is renowned for its nesting sites for several sea turtle species, including the Olive Ridley Turtle. The Rekawa Turtle Conservation Project operates in this area, providing visitors with the opportunity to witness the nesting and hatching process while ensuring the safety and conservation of the turtles.
  • Kosgoda Beach: Along the southwestern coast, Kosgoda Beach is another significant nesting site for Olive Ridley Turtles. The Kosgoda Turtle Conservation Project, operating in this area, plays a vital role in protecting these turtles and educating visitors about their conservation needs.
  • Palk Bay: Located in the northern part of Sri Lanka, Palk Bay is an ecologically diverse region that serves as a habitat for numerous marine species, including the Olive Ridley Turtle. This area is known for its rich biodiversity and is often frequented by researchers and conservationists.
  • Mannar Island: Situated in the northwestern coast, Mannar Island is a nesting ground for Olive Ridley Turtles. The sandy beaches of the island provide suitable conditions for nesting, making it an important site for conservation efforts.
  • Madu Ganga Estuary: The Madu Ganga Estuary, located in the southwestern region of Sri Lanka, is a network of interconnected waterways and mangrove forests. This estuary serves as a habitat for various wildlife, including Olive Ridley Turtles that can be spotted in the surrounding waters.

Loggerhead Turtle


The Loggerhead turtle (Caretta caretta) is a species of sea turtle found in various locations worldwide, including Sri Lanka. These turtles are characterized by their large heads and reddish-brown or yellow-brown carapace. Growing up to about three feet in length and weighing around 250 pounds on average, Loggerhead turtles possess strong swimming flippers. While they are less common than Green turtles in Sri Lanka, Loggerhead turtles can occasionally be spotted in the coastal waters. Like other sea turtles, Loggerheads engage in nesting behavior, with females coming ashore to lay their eggs on sandy beaches. However, the nesting activity of Loggerheads in Sri Lanka is comparatively less frequent.

As with all sea turtles, Loggerheads face threats such as habitat degradation, pollution, entanglement in fishing gear, and poaching. Conservation efforts are essential to protect Loggerhead turtles and their nesting sites. Local marine biologists, conservation organizations, and projects in Sri Lanka work towards monitoring, researching, and educating communities to safeguard these remarkable creatures and their habitats. If interested in observing Loggerhead turtles, it is advisable to consult with local experts who can provide specific information on sightings and responsible turtle-watching practices.

Where you have a chance to see Loggerhead Turtle

  • Palk Bay: Located in the northern part of Sri Lanka, Palk Bay is an ecologically diverse region that serves as a habitat for numerous marine species, including the Loggerhead Turtle. This area is known for its rich biodiversity and is often frequented by researchers and conservationists.
  • Mannar Island: Situated in the northwestern coast, Mannar Island is a nesting ground for Loggerhead Turtles. The sandy beaches of the island provide suitable conditions for nesting, making it an important site for conservation efforts.
  • Madu Ganga Estuary: The Madu Ganga Estuary, located in the southwestern region of Sri Lanka, is a network of interconnected waterways and mangrove forests. This estuary serves as a habitat for various wildlife, including Loggerhead Turtles that can be spotted in the surrounding waters.

Leatherback Turtle


The Leatherback turtle (Dermochelys coriacea) is a remarkable species of sea turtle known for its unique characteristics and worldwide distribution. It is the largest of all sea turtles, reaching lengths of up to 7 feet (2.13 meters) and weights of over 2,000 pounds (907 kilograms). Unlike other sea turtles, the Leatherback lacks a hard shell and instead has a leathery carapace with ridges that resemble an ancient mariner's armor.

Leatherback turtles are found in both warm and cold waters, inhabiting all major oceans. They undertake some of the longest migrations of any marine creature, with individuals traveling thousands of miles between feeding and nesting grounds. These magnificent creatures are known to dive to astonishing depths, descending to over 4,200 feet (1,280 meters) in search of their favorite prey, jellyfish.

Where you have a chance to see Leatherback Turtle

  • Kosgoda Beach: Along the southwestern coast, Kosgoda Beach is another significant nesting site for Leatherback Turtles. The Kosgoda Turtle Conservation Project, operating in this area, plays a vital role in protecting these turtles and educating visitors about their conservation needs.
  • Pigeon Island National Park: Located off the east coast of Sri Lanka near Nilaveli, Pigeon Island National Park is known for its vibrant coral reefs and diverse marine life, including Leatherback turtles. Snorkeling or diving in the crystal-clear waters of Pigeon Island provides opportunities to spot these magnificent creatures.
  • Rekawa Beach: Situated on the south coast of Sri Lanka, Rekawa Beach is renowned for its turtle nesting activity. It is one of the most important nesting sites for sea turtles, including Green turtles, in the country. Conservation organizations such as the Leatherback Turtle Watch operate guided tours to observe nesting turtles during the nesting season.
